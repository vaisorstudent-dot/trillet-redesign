  ---
  CRITICAL (3/4+ agents flagged — fix before sending)

  1. No revenue decomposition or growth trajectory

  Flagged by: Deal Screener, Due Diligence, Red Team
  The problem: $500K ARR is one number. No customer count, no tier mix, no concentration    
  disclosure, no MoM/QoQ growth rate. If the Fortune 500 telecom is 60-80% of ARR, this is a
   single-customer business dressed as a platform. The agency flywheel — the entire forward 
  thesis — has "a few dozen" agencies with zero disclosed revenue.
  The kill: An investor can't model the business. No model = no conviction = no term sheet. 
  Fix: Add a revenue breakdown line (even "X% enterprise, Y% agency" with customer count per
   tier) and a growth slope ("$X to $500K in Y months").

  2. Live site contradicts the deck

  Flagged by: Deal Screener, Due Diligence, Red Team
  The problem: trillet.ai shows $29/mo (not $59), "Pro Plan: Coming Soon" (deck presents    
  4-tier as built), and a Studio $99 tier that doesn't exist in the deck. Any investor who  
  checks the site loses trust.
  Fix: Update trillet.ai before sending the deck. This is a pre-send blocker.

  3. Agency flywheel has zero evidence

  Flagged by: Red Team (deal-breaker), Due Diligence (critical), Deal Screener (critical    
  gap)
  The problem: The entire thesis is "GHL for voice AI" — agencies sign up, white-label,     
  deploy, compound. But zero agency metrics exist: no agency count, no agency case study, no
   agency retention data, no agency activation rate. The $500K ARR appears to be entirely   
  enterprise direct sales. Red Team's kill shot: "This might be a good services business    
  masquerading as a platform investment."
  Fix: Either add agency-specific metrics (even early/directional) or address this head-on: 
  "Enterprise traction proves the product. Agency GTM is what we're raising to build." Don't
   let an investor discover the gap — name it.

  ---
  HIGH (2-3 agents flagged — fix before meeting)

  4. Stack section breaks narrative momentum

  Flagged by: Narrative Editor (#1 issue), Deal Screener
  The problem: Product → Stack → Traction. The Stack is a technical appendix inserted       
  between "what we built" and "proof it works." The heading says "Five layers. All live."   
  but one is Pilot — a micro-contradiction. The closing line ("We didn't raise to build the 
  product...") is too good to be buried in a section investors skim.
  Narrative Editor's recommendation: Merge Stack into Product as a compact row. Move the    
  closing line to the Product→Traction transition where it does maximum emotional work.     

  5. Product section carries too much pricing detail

  Flagged by: Narrative Editor, Deal Screener (borderline)
  The problem: Four tiers with exact dollars, endpoint ranges, and build fees turns Product 
  into a pricing page. "$1,497/mo + $14K build" and "$49-149/endpoint" is three numbers for 
  one tier.
  Fix: Product section = deployment architecture (who buys, how it deploys). Business Model 
  = the numbers. Or simplify to "From $59/mo to enterprise contracts."

  6. Origin story CEO/COO confusion

  Flagged by: Narrative Editor, Deal Screener
  The problem: The origin section is titled "He didn't predict the category. He lived the   
  gap." — about Ming (COO). But the team grid leads with Laurence (CEO) whose bio is about  
  healthcare SaaS. An investor asks: why isn't the guy who lived the gap the CEO?
  Fix: Either explain the CEO/COO division or restructure the grid to lead with Ming.       

  7. "20 people, $0 raised" — where's the money coming from?

  Flagged by: Red Team (serious concern)
  The problem: 15 engineers in Melbourne at even modest salaries is $1.5-2.5M/year. $500K   
  ARR doesn't cover that. Either people are on deferred comp (fragile) or there's founder   
  capital being elided.
  Fix: Be prepared to answer this in the meeting. Consider adding a line: "Self-funded.     
  Cash-flow positive on enterprise contracts." — if true.
